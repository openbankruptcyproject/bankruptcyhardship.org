Hardship Discharge in Chapter 13

A plain-language guide to 11 U.S.C. Section 1328(b) -- the provision that allows a Chapter 13 debtor to receive a discharge without completing all plan payments when circumstances beyond their control make completion impossible.

What Is a Hardship Discharge?

A hardship discharge is a discharge granted before a Chapter 13 debtor has completed all payments required under their confirmed plan. It exists because life does not always cooperate with a three-to-five-year repayment schedule. Section 1328(b) recognizes that some debtors who enter Chapter 13 in good faith encounter circumstances that make it impossible to finish.

Unlike the standard Chapter 13 discharge under Section 1328(a) -- which is granted after all plan payments are made -- a hardship discharge can be requested at any point during the plan when the debtor can show the court that completion is no longer feasible and that modification will not fix the problem.

The Three Requirements

Section 1328(b) sets three conditions that must all be met before the court will grant a hardship discharge:

1. Circumstances beyond the debtor's control. The debtor's failure to complete plan payments must be due to circumstances for which the debtor should not justly be held accountable. Common examples include job loss, permanent disability, death of a spouse, serious medical emergencies, and natural disasters.

2. The best interest test is satisfied. Unsecured creditors must have received at least as much as they would have received in a hypothetical Chapter 7 liquidation. This is the same "best interest of creditors" test applied at plan confirmation under Section 1325(a)(4).

3. Modification is not practicable. The court must find that modifying the plan under Section 1329 is not a workable alternative. If a simple payment reduction or term extension could solve the problem, the court will typically deny the hardship discharge and direct the debtor to seek modification instead.

What Qualifies as Hardship?

Courts have recognized many types of hardship as sufficient under Section 1328(b). The most common include:

Job loss or income reduction -- involuntary termination, company closure, or a permanent reduction in hours or wages that makes the current payment level unsustainable.

Permanent disability -- a physical or mental condition that prevents the debtor from earning the income projected in the plan.

Serious illness or medical emergency -- major health events that consume resources and reduce the debtor's ability to work, especially when they generate new medical debt.

Death of a spouse -- loss of a co-debtor's income that was factored into the plan payment calculation.

Natural disaster -- destruction of property or displacement that disrupts the debtor's financial stability.

Incarceration -- some courts have granted hardship discharges when the debtor is incarcerated through no fault of their own (e.g., wrongful conviction later overturned).

Temporary setbacks generally do not qualify. If the debtor's situation is likely to improve, the court will usually expect the debtor to seek a plan modification rather than a hardship discharge. The key question is whether the hardship is permanent or long-term enough that no reasonable modification can cure the shortfall.

Hardship Discharge vs. Full Discharge

The most important difference between a hardship discharge under Section 1328(b) and a full discharge under Section 1328(a) is the scope of debts discharged.

A full Chapter 13 discharge (Section 1328(a)) is sometimes called the "superdischarge" because it covers some debts that would survive a Chapter 7 case. For example, debts from willful and malicious injury to property (but not persons) can be discharged under 1328(a) but not under Chapter 7.

A hardship discharge (Section 1328(b)) does not include the superdischarge. It only discharges debts that would be dischargeable in a Chapter 7 case. This means debts under Section 523(a) -- including debts obtained by fraud (523(a)(2)), debts for fraud or defalcation in a fiduciary capacity (523(a)(4)), and debts for willful and malicious injury (523(a)(6)) -- all survive a hardship discharge, just as they would survive a Chapter 7 discharge.

This narrower scope is the trade-off for getting out of the plan early. Debtors should weigh whether the remaining nondischargeable debts are significant before requesting a hardship discharge.

How to Request a Hardship Discharge

File a motion with the bankruptcy court. The debtor must file a motion requesting a hardship discharge under Section 1328(b). The motion should explain the changed circumstances and address each of the three statutory requirements.

Provide evidence of changed circumstances. Attach supporting documentation -- medical records, termination letters, disability determinations, death certificates, or other evidence showing the hardship is real and beyond the debtor's control.

Demonstrate that modification is not practicable. Explain why reducing payments or extending the plan term will not solve the problem. If the debtor's income has dropped to zero or near zero, this element is straightforward. If some income remains, the debtor should show that even a modified plan would fail.

Show the best interest test is met. Provide a calculation showing that unsecured creditors have already received at least what they would have gotten in a Chapter 7 liquidation. In many consumer cases -- where the debtor has few non-exempt assets -- this requirement is easily satisfied because creditors would have received little or nothing in Chapter 7.

The court will typically schedule a hearing on the motion. The Chapter 13 trustee and creditors have the right to object.

Alternatives to a Hardship Discharge

Before filing for a hardship discharge, consider whether one of these alternatives might work:

Plan modification (Section 1329) -- if the hardship is temporary or partial, modifying the plan to reduce payments or extend the term (up to the statutory maximum) may be a better option. Learn more at chapter13plan.org.

Conversion to Chapter 7 -- if the debtor qualifies, converting the case to Chapter 7 may provide a faster path to discharge. However, conversion comes with its own trade-offs, including potential asset liquidation. See whatischapter7.com.

Voluntary dismissal -- dismissing the case ends the plan obligation but provides no discharge. The debtor returns to pre-bankruptcy status with creditors.

The full Chapter 13 discharge under Section 1328(a) remains the best outcome when possible. For more on Section 1328 generally, visit section1328.org.
